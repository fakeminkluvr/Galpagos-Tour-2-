Part One

Charles Darwin and the Voyage of HMS Beagle

In 1831, Charles Darwin (aged 22) was offered the position of naturalist aboard HMS Beagle, a Royal Navy survey ship that was about to travel around the world. The voyage lasted nearly five years and changed how scientists understood life on Earth.

Darwin spent the journey observing and collecting specimens across South America, the Pacific Islands and beyond. His observations started to challenge the idea that species were fixed and never changed.

Route of HMS Beagle (1831-1836)

Plymouth, England -> Cape Verde -> Brazil -> Argentina -> Chile -> Galapagos Islands -> Tahiti -> New Zealand -> Australia -> England

HMS Beagle arrived at the Galapagos on 15 September 1835. Darwin spent about five weeks across four islands (San Cristobal, Floreana, Isabela and Santiago) before the ship left on 20 October 1835.

Figure 1: The route of HMS Beagle during Darwin's 1831–1836 voyage. The Galapagos Islands (red star) were visited from 15 September to 20 October 1835 — the key turning point of the voyage.

Key observations Darwin made

San Cristobal
Tortoises with different shell shapes The local vice-governor told Darwin he could identify which island a tortoise came from just by looking at its shell. Dome-shaped shells on humid islands, saddle-backed shells on dry islands. Darwin did not think much of this at the time, but it became an important clue later.
Floreana and other islands
Mockingbirds that were slightly different on each island Darwin collected mockingbirds from several islands and noticed they were consistently different from each other. This was an early hint that populations could change depending on where they lived.
Multiple islands
Finches with different beak shapes Darwin collected around 70 bird specimens including 13 finch species, though he did not realise how significant they were at the time. Back in London, ornithologist John Gould identified them all as finches, each adapted to a different food source. This was a major turning point.
Marine environments
Marine iguanas that swim and feed underwater Darwin observed iguanas that had evolved to swim and feed on algae growing on underwater rocks. No other lizard in the world does this. They showed how dramatically a population can adapt to local conditions.

How Darwin developed his theory

Darwin did not have a sudden "eureka" moment in the Galapagos. The theory came together gradually after he returned to England in 1836. When John Gould confirmed that all the different birds Darwin collected were actually related finches, it was a key turning point.

Darwin also read Thomas Malthus's essay on population, which argued that populations always grow faster than their food supply, causing competition. Darwin applied this idea to nature: individuals with traits that help them survive are more likely to reproduce and pass on those traits, slowly changing the population over time.

Darwin spent over 20 years refining his theory before publishing On the Origin of Species in 1859.

The four steps of natural selection

1

Variation

Individuals within a population show heritable differences in their traits.

2

Overproduction

Populations produce more offspring than the environment can support.

3

Competition

Individuals compete for limited food, space and other resources.

4

Differential Survival

Individuals with useful traits survive and pass those traits on to offspring.

Part Two

A Three-Island Educational Tour

The tour visits three islands chosen because each one shows a different aspect of evolution and adaptation. Total duration is 9 days.

Route: Santa Cruz (Days 1–3) → Espanola (Days 4–6) → Isabela (Days 7–9)

Detailed Day-by-Day Itinerary
Day 1 Santa Cruz
Arrive Puerto Ayora · Charles Darwin Research Station
Fly into Baltra Airport and transfer by boat to Puerto Ayora. Afternoon visit to the Charles Darwin Research Station to observe the giant tortoise breeding programme and speak to conservation scientists.
Day 2 Santa Cruz
Santa Cruz Highlands · Tortoise reserve · Vegetation zones
Full-day hike through the six vegetation zones of Santa Cruz — from dry coastal scrub to humid scalesia forest — observing wild giant tortoises. Study how Prickly Pear Cacti grow taller on tortoise-inhabited islands as an evolved defence.
Day 3 Santa Cruz
Las Grietas snorkelling · Depart for Espanola
Morning snorkelling at Las Grietas lava fissures to observe marine life. Afternoon speedboat transfer to Espanola (approx. 3 hours). Brief orientation walk at Gardner Bay on arrival.
Day 4 Espanola
Punta Suarez · Waved Albatross colony
Full-day visit to Punta Suarez. Observe the only Waved Albatross breeding colony in the world (approx. 12,000 pairs nest here seasonally). Watch synchronised courtship dances and study how the 2.4 m wingspan enables efficient soaring over the Pacific.
Day 5 Espanola
Marine iguanas · Gardner Bay snorkelling
Morning observation of Espanola's Marine Iguanas — identifiable by their red and green colouring during breeding season. Observe salt-sneezing and communal basking. Afternoon snorkelling at Gardner Bay alongside sea lions and reef fish.
Day 6 Espanola
Allopatric speciation workshop · Depart for Isabela
Morning guided discussion using Espanola's endemic species as case studies for allopatric speciation — how geographic isolation creates new species over millions of years. Afternoon boat transfer to Isabela (approx. 2.5 hours), arriving at Puerto Villamil.
Day 7 Isabela
Tintoreras · Galapagos Penguin colony
Morning boat tour around the Tintoreras islets to observe Galapagos Penguins — the only penguin species found north of the equator. Compare their heat management adaptations to cold-climate penguin species. Snorkel alongside penguins and white-tipped reef sharks.
Day 8 Isabela
Flightless Cormorant walk · Sierra Negra volcano
Morning coastal walk to observe Flightless Cormorants drying their vestigial wings — a study in relaxed selection when no land predators exist. Afternoon hike to the rim of Sierra Negra, one of Isabela's five active volcanoes, to observe primary succession on fresh lava.
Day 9 Isabela
Tortoise breeding centre · Depart
Morning visit to Isabela's Giant Tortoise Breeding Centre to compare the five different tortoise subspecies — one per volcano — demonstrating how lava-flow barriers drive diversification within a single island. Ferry to Baltra Airport and depart.

Santa Cruz

Days 1-3
Also known as: Indefatigable Island Central Galapagos Charles Darwin Research Station Multiple vegetation zones
Why this island was chosen Santa Cruz has the highest biodiversity of any Galapagos island, with habitats ranging from dry coastal scrub to humid highland forest. It is also home to the Charles Darwin Research Station which runs the tortoise breeding programme. This makes it ideal for understanding how evolution and conservation are connected.

Students can observe giant tortoises in their natural highland habitat and speak to scientists at the research station about current conservation work.

Featured organisms

Organism Adaptation type Explanation
Galapagos Giant Tortoise
Chelonoidis niger		 Structural Physiological Behavioural Dome-shaped shells on humid islands, saddle-backed shells on dry islands where the neck needs to stretch up to reach cacti. Tortoises can also survive up to a year without food or water, and they migrate between highland and lowland areas to regulate body temperature and access food.
Prickly Pear Cactus
Opuntia echios		 Structural Physiological On islands with tortoises and iguanas, the cactus evolved to grow tall with a woody trunk so the edible pads are out of reach of ground animals. It also uses CAM photosynthesis, opening its stomata at night instead of during the day to reduce water loss.

Espanola

Days 4-6
Also known as: Hood Island Oldest island in the archipelago Highest endemism rate of any Galapagos island Rocky coastlines and seabird colonies
Why this island was chosen Espanola is the oldest island in the archipelago and has been geographically isolated for millions of years. This has resulted in the highest rate of endemic species of any Galapagos island. It is a good case study for allopatric speciation, which is when isolated populations gradually become separate species.

Espanola shows what happens when populations are isolated for long enough. The organisms here have changed so much from their ancestors that they are now completely different species.

Featured organisms

Organism Adaptation type Explanation
Waved Albatross
Phoebastria irrorata		 Structural Physiological Behavioural A wingspan of up to 2.4 m lets the albatross soar over the ocean for months at a time, using wind gradients to travel long distances with minimal energy. Physiologically, albatrosses have a specialised nasal tube that enhances their sense of smell, allowing them to detect food sources like fish and squid across hundreds of kilometres of open ocean — a critical survival advantage in a featureless environment. Albatrosses also perform complex synchronised dances to form long-term mating pairs, which improves offspring survival rates.
Marine Iguana
Amblyrhynchus cristatus		 Structural Physiological Behavioural A flat tail acts as a rudder for swimming, and a blunt downward-facing snout scrapes algae off underwater rocks. Specialised nasal glands remove excess salt by sneezing it out through the nostrils. After diving in cold water, iguanas bask together on lava rocks to warm up faster.

Isabela

Days 7-9
Also known as: Albemarle Island Largest Galapagos island Five active shield volcanoes Only island where Galapagos penguins breed
Why this island was chosen Isabela is the largest and most volcanically active island. Its five volcanoes are separated by lava flows, so each supports a different tortoise subspecies. This makes it like a miniature version of the whole Galapagos. Fresh lava flows also let scientists study how life colonises new environments from scratch, which is useful for understanding early evolution on isolated islands.

Featured organisms

Organism Adaptation type Explanation
Galapagos Penguin
Spheniscus mendiculus		 Structural Physiological Behavioural Unlike Antarctic penguins, Galapagos penguins have thinner feathers to avoid overheating in a tropical climate. Their moult is timed to match food-rich periods linked to the Humboldt Current. On hot days they hold their flippers out from their body to lose heat and they seek shade under rocks.
Flightless Cormorant
Nannopterum harrisi		 Structural Physiological Behavioural The only cormorant in the world that cannot fly. With no land predators on Isabela, large flight muscles had no survival benefit, so over many generations wings became much smaller — a structural change driven by relaxed selection. Physiologically, the cormorant has a higher concentration of oxygen-storing myoglobin in its muscles compared to flying cormorant species, improving its breath-holding capacity and diving efficiency in the cold Humboldt Current upwellings. Behaviourally, the birds still hold their vestigial wings out to dry after diving, a retained ancestral behaviour that no longer serves its original aerodynamic purpose.

Part Three

The Grants' Study: Evolution Observed in Real Time

Peter and Rosemary Grant from Princeton University conducted one of the most important long-term studies in evolutionary biology. Starting in 1973, they spent decades on Daphne Major, a small volcanic island in the Galapagos, studying Darwin's finches. Their work was the first time scientists directly measured natural selection happening in a wild population.

40+ Years of continuous study
18,000+ Individual birds banded and tracked
0.5 mm Average beak depth increase after 1977 drought

Key findings

The 1977 drought (abiotic factor): A severe drought wiped out most small, soft seeds which were the preferred food of medium ground finches. Only large, hard seeds were left. Finches with bigger, deeper beaks could crack these seeds more easily and survived at higher rates. The average beak depth increased by about 0.5 mm in just one generation. This was caused by a change in rainfall, which is an abiotic factor.

The 1983 El Nino reversal (abiotic factor): Heavy rain brought lots of small seeds back. Smaller-beaked finches now had an advantage because they could eat more efficiently. Beak size shifted back toward smaller. This showed that natural selection can go in reverse when conditions change.

Competition between species (biotic factor): In 2004, a drought happened at the same time a larger finch species (Geospiza magnirostris) arrived on Daphne Major. The larger species outcompeted the medium ground finch for big seeds, so selection now favoured smaller beaks. This showed that biotic factors like competition can drive natural selection just as strongly as abiotic factors.

Why this study matters

Before the Grants' research, most scientists assumed natural selection was too slow to observe within a human lifetime — it was thought to take thousands or millions of years. The Grants proved this assumption wrong. Their study was the first time natural selection was directly measured in a wild population as it occurred, making it one of the most important pieces of evidence for evolutionary theory ever produced.

By measuring nearly every bird on Daphne Major for over 40 years, they showed that selection pressure from a single environmental event — such as a drought — can cause statistically significant, heritable changes in a population in under two years. Crucially, the study also demonstrated that evolution is not a one-way process: populations can shift in one direction and then reverse when conditions change, as seen in the 1977 drought followed by the 1983 El Niño.

The Grants' work directly confirmed Darwin's theory of natural selection using real measurements from a wild population, and it showed that both abiotic factors (like rainfall and temperature) and biotic factors (like competition between species) can independently drive evolutionary change. Their findings remain a cornerstone of modern evolutionary biology.
